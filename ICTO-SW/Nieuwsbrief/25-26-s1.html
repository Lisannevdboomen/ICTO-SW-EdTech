Connect your own laptop

Starting in September, desktops will no longer be available in workgroup rooms. Instead, you will need to use your own (UvA) laptop or personal laptop to connect to the audiovisual equipment. Connections are available via HDMI and USB-C. Desktops will still be available in practical rooms and lecture halls.

We recommend testing your laptop before your first class or lecture to familiarize yourself with the system. It's plug and play, with clear instructions posted next to the control panel in the room. If you encounter any issues, AV support is available (contact details are on the control panel in the workgroup rooms).

UvA launches its own AI chat

The UvA AI Chat has been specially developed to let users safely explore generative AI tools, such as ChatGPT, in education. Unlike commercial platforms, all data shared in this chat stays within UvA systems and is processed anonymously.

Staff already had access, and from 1 September, all UvA students will be able to use UvA AI Chat to experiment with AI safely and responsibly in their studies.

The launch is part of a phased rollout of GenAI at UvA, with the first phase focused on educational use. More information about the principles, rules, and rollout phases of GenAI at UvA can be found on the UvA website and the staff portal.

Peerceptiv

Peerceptiv is a powerful tool for organising peer review and peer assessment in your course. It allows students to provide structured, anonymous feedback on written work, images, videos, or even live presentations. Below, some of Peerceptiv's possibilities are described.

  • Set up individual or group peer reviews
  • Create sub-groups for targeted reviewing
  • Enable team member evaluations within projects
  • Collect feedback during live presentations

Because feedback is anonymous, students feel more comfortable being honest, while also learning to evaluate the work of others. Interested in getting started? Visit the Canvas page for UvA instructors for more information and step-by-step instructions, or watch the video below.

Copyright in Education: What You Need to Know

When creating teaching materials, you may want to include excerpts from books, journal articles, or images. The UvA participates in the national Easy Access Agreement (2023–2025), which sets clear rules for using copyrighted material in non-commercial education. Under this agreement, you can freely reuse:

  • Up to 40 pages from a single publication, as long as it doesn’t exceed 20% of the work (before 2023, the limit was 50 pages, so double-check older materials!).
  • Up to 10 works by the same creator (e.g. images, illustrations, artworks).
  • Up to 25 images from a single work.

These limits apply both digitally and on paper - whether you upload to Canvas, share in Teams, create a reader, or use slides in class. As long as you stay within the limits, you can download and upload material directly, provided you always cite the source.

Need to use more? You have two options:

  1. Request permission via the UvA library, which is part of the national Copyright Information Points (AIP) network and can arrange access through Stichting UvO. Ask the library.
  2. Link to the source instead of uploading. This is preferred whenever possible, as it avoids copyright transfer and reduces risk for the university.

Open data

Did you know that the UvA Library not only shares its own datasets openly but also provides UvA and HvA researchers with a platform to make their research data publicly available?

Open data refers to datasets that are freely accessible and reusable—an important part of the global Open Science movement. By publishing data, researchers promote transparency, collaboration, and new discoveries across disciplines. Some examples of UvA’s open datasets include:

  • Cinema Context (Dutch film culture since 1896)
  • Onstage (17th-18th century Amsterdam theatre records)
  • Ecartico (biographical data on Amsterdam’s cultural scene in the Golden Age)

Interested in how open data could enhance your teaching or research? Or curious about publishing your own datasets? The library offers an e-learning module on open data where you can explore the basics at your own pace. It’s a great way to discover new resources for your courses or make your own research more visible.

Protecting Privacy in Digital Education

As educators, handling students’ and colleagues’ personal data responsibly is essential. In line with the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), it’s important to avoid storing sensitive information on personal devices, such as laptops or smartphones.

Instead, always use your university laptop and the secure tools provided in Office 365 to store and manage personal data. When sharing information about student groups, share only what is strictly necessary. Avoid sending full email lists or other sensitive data via email. Use secure platforms and limit the information shared to protect everyone’s privacy.
